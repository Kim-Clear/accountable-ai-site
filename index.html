The problem

The risk is not only that AI gets
something wrong.

The greater risk is that the organisation cannot later reconstruct how the outcome was reached, what was checked, or whether review was realistically possible.

Who checked this output? What source evidence was visible? Was the reviewer able to challenge it? What changed between the AI draft and the final action?

These are the questions that matter when an AI-assisted outcome is challenged.

"Human-in-the-loop" is not enough on its own.

Review depends on time, evidence visibility, escalation routes, and whether uncertainty can be recorded.

The gap between nominal oversight and operationally meaningful review is where defensibility often breaks down.

The method

The Five-Question
Defensibility Test

We use a simple five-question test to assess whether your AI use would stand up under scrutiny. This is not a policy review or a generic governance workshop. It is a practical way to examine real AI use cases and understand where decision-making is clear, where accountability sits, and where the process may break down.

Clarity

What is the AI actually doing?

Impact

What decision or action follows from the output?

Ownership

Who is accountable?

Risk

What happens if it is wrong?

Review

How is it checked before action is taken?

The result is a clearer view of what is defensible, what is exposed, and what needs to change first.

About

Kim Stevens

My background is in regulated environments — including clinical research and global pharmaceutical settings — where accountability is not optional.

Accountable AI focuses on the operational conditions around AI use: what people can see, what they can check, what gets recorded, and whether a decision can be defended after the fact.

I have worked directly with AI tools in professional settings — including supporting the evaluation and rollout of Microsoft Copilot — and seen how quickly the gap between everyday use and genuine oversight develops.

Accountability has to survive contact with the workflow. That means looking beyond policy language and into the conditions under which people actually review, accept, edit, escalate, or action AI-assisted outputs.
