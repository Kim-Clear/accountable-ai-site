The problem

AI-supported work often moves faster than
accountability conditions.

When AI touches data, informs a decision, or shapes client-facing work, the key questions are operational: who reviewed it, what evidence was visible, what changed because of the output, and when escalation should have happened.

Who checked this output? What data was involved? What action followed? What would happen if it was wrong — and who owns the outcome?

These questions are not abstract governance questions. They determine whether the workflow can be explained after the fact.

"Human oversight" only helps if the review is meaningful.

A quick glance, undocumented approval, or unclear handoff is not a reliable control. BAT looks at the practical review conditions: what the person saw, what they checked, and what route existed if the work needed to stop.

About

Kim Stevens

My background is in regulated environments — including clinical research and global pharmaceutical settings — where accountability is not optional.

That background shapes how I approach AI. I do not see it as a technology problem first, but as a decision and accountability problem. Who owns what. What happens when something is wrong. Whether the use of AI in a process would actually hold up if examined.

I have also worked directly with AI tools in professional settings — including supporting the evaluation and rollout of Microsoft Copilot — and seen firsthand how the gap between everyday use and genuine oversight develops.

AI-supported work is only defensible when the workflow conditions are visible. That means understanding ownership, evidence, review steps, and escalation routes.

That is what Accountable AI is built to address — operational accountability, meaningful human oversight, and defensibility under scrutiny.
