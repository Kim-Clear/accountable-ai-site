AIM Framework

A decision gate for AI actions —
not just a policy after the fact

AIM, the Authority & Intervention Model, is a practical framework for deciding whether an AI system should be allowed to act at the moment of execution.

It helps answer one question clearly:

Can this action happen right now — and why?

Ask about AIM See how it works
The gap

AI governance often stops too early

Many organisations already have AI policies, review processes, and lifecycle controls.

Those all matter. But they do not always answer the operational question that matters most in practice:

Should this system be allowed to do this specific thing now, on this basis, at this level of consequence?

That is the gap AIM is designed to address.

AIM focuses on the point where an AI system is about to act. It makes the decision gate visible, testable, and easier to defend.

What AIM does

A runtime decision-control model for AI systems and agent workflows

AIM evaluates four things:

01
Action
What is the system about to do?
  • send a communication
  • update a record
  • approve or reject something
  • change access rights
02
Authority
What gives it permission to do that?
  • delegated business rules
  • defined task scope
  • policy thresholds
  • human approval on file
03
Basis
What is the action relying on?
  • user input
  • source material
  • workflow or CRM data
  • specialist review findings
04
Impact
What happens if it is wrong?
  • operational disruption
  • customer harm
  • regulatory exposure
  • irreversibility
Runtime outcome

What AIM returns

Each action evaluated by AIM resolves to one of five states.

Allow
Authority confirmed. Basis sufficient. Impact within bounds.
Allow with review
Proceed with visibility. Revalidation needed before the next step.
Block
Do not proceed. Escalation or redesign is required.
Escalate
A human decision is required before the workflow can continue.
Hold until cleared
A specialist finding is active. Downstream execution pauses until it is resolved.
Multi-agent workflows

Why step-level checks are not enough

In multi-agent systems, the challenge is not only whether one step is acceptable. It is also whether the chain as a whole remains defensible as work moves between agents.

Chain sequencing
A blocked upstream step should stop the chain
If the chain is already blocked upstream, downstream steps should not continue as if nothing happened.
Inherited corroboration
Agreement is not the same as verification
Repeated agreement is not the same as independent verification. A chain should not become more trusted simply because several agents endorse the same weak basis.
Specialist hold handling
A local pass can still create a downstream hold
A specialist review can pass locally and still create a downstream hold if it identifies a real unresolved issue.
Who AIM is for

Where AIM is most useful

AIM is designed for organisations using AI in workflows where decisions need to remain visible, explainable, and controlled.

Particularly relevant for
  • AI-enabled workflow design
  • copilots and internal automation
  • governance, risk, and compliance teams
  • product, operations, and programme leads
  • regulated or higher-risk environments
Especially useful when AI outputs can affect
  • customers
  • records
  • approvals
  • access
  • pricing
  • regulated content
  • operational decisions
Practical value

What AIM helps you do

Identify where AI actions should be allowed, reviewed, blocked, or held
Map decision gates inside live workflows
Distinguish weak basis from genuine verification
Define stop points and escalation routes
Make specialist findings visible in operational control
Strengthen the defensibility of AI-enabled processes
Deployment

From workflow review to operational embed

AIM can be applied at different stages depending on where a team is in their AI workflow development.

01
Framework deployment / workflow review
For teams that need to review an AI-enabled workflow, identify control gaps, and define decision points.
Typical outputs
Workflow map, action scoring, decision gates, stop points, governance recommendations, operating model.
02
Prototype control tool
For teams that want a repeatable control method before building software.
Typical outputs
Workbook, rules sheet, scenario library, reusable assessment method, templates.
03
Operational embed / integration
For organisations running live AI in regulated or high-risk workflows.
Typical outputs
Pre-action checks, hold and escalation handling, audit trail, exception flows, policy enforcement logic.
Example in practice

When a workflow should stop

A marketing workflow prepares a customer segment, generates campaign content, runs compliance review, and then attempts to send externally.

AIM can distinguish between:

  • a content step that proceeds with caution because it relies on a partial basis
  • a compliance step that is locally valid but raises a hold
  • a remediation step that is allowed while the hold is active
  • a final send step that must be blocked because the hold has not been cleared

That matters because weak basis, false reassurance, unresolved specialist findings, and final execution risk are not the same problem. They need different responses.

Clear boundaries

What AIM is not

  • Not a generic AI policy
  • Not a retrospective audit only
  • Not a replacement for human accountability
  • Not a finished software platform
  • Not a promise of unrestricted automation
AIM is a practical decision-control model that can be used now as a review method and prototype control tool, and later embedded more operationally where needed.
The point of AIM

AI governance is not only about whether a system has been approved in general

It is also about whether a specific action is justified at the moment the system is about to take it. AIM is designed to make that visible, testable, and controllable.
