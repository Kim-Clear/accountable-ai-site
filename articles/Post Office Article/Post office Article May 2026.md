The Post Office Had Humans in the Loop Too

May 30, 2026

“Human-in-the-loop” is one of the most reassuring phrases in AI governance. It suggests there is still a person in the chain. Someone able to apply judgement, question the output and stop harm before it becomes a decision.

The Horizon scandal shows why that assumption needs scrutiny.

Horizon was not an AI system. It was an accounting platform used by the Post Office. The technologies and legal context differ. The structural risk does not.

The point is not that AI systems and Horizon are equivalent. They are not. The point is that organisations can make the same accountability mistake with very different technologies: treating system outputs as reliable, treating challenge as exceptional, and treating human involvement as proof that responsibility has been managed.

From 1999 onwards, Horizon-generated shortfalls were treated as authoritative. Sub-postmasters were required to repay apparent deficits and, in many cases, were prosecuted for theft, fraud or false accounting. More than 900 people were prosecuted. Many convictions have since been overturned as evidence of system faults emerged.

Humans were involved throughout. They reviewed accounts, conducted investigations, authorised prosecutions and assessed the credibility of those challenging the system. Their presence did not prevent large-scale injustice.

That does not make human oversight irrelevant. It makes its design decisive.

### What failed in practice
Viewed as an accountability case, several features stand out.

System outputs were institutionally privileged. Discrepancies were framed as debts owed unless disproven. In practice, the burden shifted to individuals who could not meaningfully interrogate the system.

Decision-makers did not appear to have effective capacity to test Horizon at source. Their role was largely to act on its outputs rather than independently scrutinise them.

Repeated concerns were treated as isolated disputes rather than potential systemic failure. Escalation did not reliably trigger structural review.

As prosecutions proceeded, acknowledging error became institutionally costly. Organisational defensiveness compounded technical risk.

None of this required full automation. It required deference to a technical system combined with constrained challenge and increasing institutional commitment.

### The relevance to AI-supported decisions

In contemporary AI deployments, “human-in-the-loop” is often cited to show that decisions are not automated and therefore remain accountable.

Horizon suggests a more practical test.

> At the point of reliance, what was the human empowered to do?


Could they examine underlying data and reasoning? Could they pause or block reliance? Were incentives aligned with scrutiny or throughput? Was uncertainty visible and recordable? Did challenge trigger independent review?

If the human role is limited to confirming or processing system outputs, accountability remains structurally weak. The human becomes the final procedural step in a chain of reliance rather than an independent control.

### Institutional trust and burden shifting

A defining feature of Horizon was institutional trust in system outputs. When individuals asserted that the system was wrong, the presumption favoured the technology. Those affected were required to disprove a system they could not inspect.

The same dynamic can arise in AI-supported contexts. If a risk score, eligibility classification or prioritisation outcome is treated as presumptively correct, the burden may shift to the affected person to demonstrate error. Where systems are complex or opaque, that burden may be unrealistic.

The formal presence of a human decision-maker does not correct this imbalance if that decision-maker operates under the same presumption.

### What meaningful oversight requires

Horizon illustrates what credible oversight must include.

Decision-makers need access to sufficient information about how outputs were produced and their limitations.

They need authority to depart from system outputs without disproportionate personal or organisational cost.

There must be a credible escalation route for systemic concerns.

And there must be a record of what happened at the decision point: what the system indicated, what was reviewed, what was accepted or rejected, and why.

Accountability does not sit in policy statements. It sits in the workflow.

If a system output influences a consequential decision, there must be clarity about what was relied on, what evidence was available, what discretion was exercised and what would have happened if the output had been rejected. Those elements need to be structured into the process and recorded at the moment of reliance.

Without that, “human involvement” describes form rather than accountability.

### Implications for AI governance

For organisations deploying AI in consequential settings, human involvement should not be treated as the control. The control is the workflow around that human: what they can see, what they can challenge, what they can stop and what is recorded.

If challenged later, can the organisation show:

- what the system contributed 
- what evidence was available 
- what uncertainty was known 
- what discretion was exercised 
- what would have happened if the output had been rejected

If those elements cannot be evidenced, accountability rests on assertion rather than record.

The Horizon case demonstrates how institutional confidence in a system can outpace the evidence supporting it, and how difficult it becomes to reverse course once reliance is embedded.

### Conclusion

The Horizon scandal should not be used loosely as a metaphor for AI risk. It was a specific failure involving specific technologies and organisational behaviour.

But it provides a disciplined lesson.

> Human presence in a workflow does not guarantee oversight. Oversight depends on authority, evidence, independence and credible challenge routes.

When organisations rely on “human-in-the-loop” as proof of responsible AI use, they should be clear about what that human can actually do, what they can see, and what power they hold when the system is wrong.

That is where accountability either survives scrutiny or collapses.
