##Boeing 737 MAX and the Limits of “The Human Can Intervene”
Kim Stevens

June 2, 2026

"The human can intervene" is one of the most reassuring things an organisation can say about a safety-critical system. It suggests there is still a backstop. If automation behaves unexpectedly, a person will notice, take control and prevent harm before the failure becomes a real-world consequence. The Boeing 737 MAX crashes show why that reassurance deserves closer scrutiny. This is not an AI story. MCAS was not a machine learning system, and aviation has its own technical, operational and regulatory context. The point is not to turn every technology failure into an AI analogy. The lesson is narrower than that, and more useful: a human can be present in the loop and still be poorly placed to intervene, if the system design removes the time, information or control context they need to act well. That distinction matters for AI governance.

### What MCAS actually shows 
The Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System was designed to adjust aircraft handling in specific flight conditions. Before it was redesigned, MCAS could rely on input from a single angle-of-attack sensor. In the Lion Air and Ethiopian Airlines accidents, incorrect sensor data caused it to activate repeatedly. The pilots were not outside the loop. They were trained professionals, working inside a system that still expected them to recover from unexpected behaviour under extreme pressure. But that is not the same as saying the design gave them enough visibility, time or control context to intervene well. Human intervention only works as a safeguard when the conditions for it actually exist. The person needs to see what the system is doing, not only experience the consequences of what it has already done. They need enough context to recognise when behaviour is abnormal, training for the failure modes they may actually face rather than just the expected workflow, and enough time to diagnose and respond. They also need clear authority when automation and human judgement conflict. When those conditions are missing, "human intervention" is a theoretical safety argument. In practice, the person is simply being asked to compensate for a design problem under pressure.

### Presence is not empowerment 
When an organisation says "a human can intervene," that claim needs to be tested practically, not taken at face value. It is not enough that a person is formally present, or that a process document says they retain responsibility. The useful question is what the person can actually see and do at the point where intervention is needed. Can they see what the system is doing? Can they understand why it is behaving that way? Can they recognise that something has gone wrong soon enough for it to matter? Can they pause the process, override the system or escalate without being penalised for slowing things down? These are not small operational details. They determine whether the human is acting as a meaningful control or simply carrying formal responsibility for a system-shaped outcome. A person may technically remain accountable while being structurally set up to fail. That matters because human involvement is routinely offered as reassurance in governance conversations. The person becomes the safety layer, but if the workflow does not give them visibility, time and real authority, that safety layer may exist mostly on paper.

The problem with AI-supported decisions
AI deployments lean on a similar reassurance. The model recommends, flags, scores, prioritises or summarises, but a human makes the final decision. That may be true but it may also be weak governance. A reviewer can be present and still be unable to challenge the system in any meaningful way. They may see the output but not the basis for it. They may be expected to approve quickly. They may not know what failure modes to look for. They may be measured on throughput rather than scrutiny. They may have no realistic route to pause or escalate. And while disagreement might be technically allowed, it can be socially, operationally or commercially discouraged. In that setup, human review becomes fragile. The human is in the workflow, but the workflow was never designed around meaningful intervention. This is where AI governance needs to move beyond the comfort of human presence. The question is not only whether a person remains somewhere in the process. What matters is whether the person has a realistic opportunity to understand what is happening, challenge it where needed and stop reliance before harm is done.

### Timing matters more than people assume 
In aviation, the critical moment can arrive in seconds. Most AI-supported decisions unfold more slowly - recruitment screening, eligibility assessments, healthcare triage, safeguarding alerts, financial risk scoring. There is rarely physical danger measured in moments. Timing still matters, just differently. If an AI output has already shaped the framing, narrowed the options or pushed a case down a particular path, the human may be intervening too late by the time they reach the decision point. The system may already have defined what seems relevant, what appears risky, what looks normal and what options feel available. The organisation can still say that a human made the final decision. But that does not answer the harder question: how much of the decision environment had already been structured by the system before the human was asked to approve, reject or review it? That is where human oversight can quietly turn into delayed accountability.

### The assumptions built into the design 
Every human-in-the-loop design contains assumptions about the person in the loop. Sometimes those assumptions are explicit. Often they are not. The person may be expected to notice a subtle failure, identify uncertainty the system does not surface, challenge a confident output, slow down a process designed for speed or override automation without institutional backing. Those expectations are part of the safety design and they should not sit quietly in the background as unexamined hopes. If a system relies on humans correcting it, the organisation has to design for that correction. That means providing the information, time, authority and support needed for correction to be realistic. Otherwise the human becomes a comforting line in a document while being underpowered in the actual workflow.

### What accountability actually requires 
"Human-in-the-loop" is not a control by itself. The control is the workflow around the human: what they can see, what they can challenge, what they can stop, what gets recorded and what happens when they disagree with the system. Those questions become decisive when an outcome is later challenged. A human sign-off does not show much on its own. If the outcome is challenged later, the organisation needs evidence of what the system contributed, what the human reviewed, what uncertainty was visible, whether the reviewer could depart from the output, and what would have happened if reliance had been rejected If those elements cannot be demonstrated, accountability rests on assertion rather than record. For consequential AI use, that is a weak position.

### Conclusion 
The MCAS failures are not an AI case and should not be stretched into one, but they offer a disciplined lesson for human-in-the-loop governance. A human can be present and still be underpowered by the design around them. Intervention depends on visibility, time, training, authority and control context. For AI-supported decisions, those conditions cannot be assumed from the presence of a person in the process. They have to be built into the workflow before anyone relies on the output.
